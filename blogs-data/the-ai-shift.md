Over the last two years, development workflows have changed more than in the previous decade. AI-assisted coding, automated testing, and intelligent code review are no longer experimental — they are becoming standard practice in forward-thinking engineering teams.

## The Speed Paradox

There is a paradox at the heart of AI-assisted development. Teams are moving faster than ever. Engineers who have integrated AI tools into their workflows report significant productivity gains. But speed without clarity creates its own problems.

When junior engineers can generate working code without fully understanding what it does, technical debt accumulates in ways that are harder to identify and address. When teams deploy faster, the gap between what is built and what is tested can widen. When AI handles more of the routine work, the skills that come from doing that routine work — pattern recognition, debugging instinct, architectural intuition — can atrophy.

## The Talent Gap Risk

The risk is subtle but real. A generation of engineers who have learned primarily through AI-assisted workflows may find that certain foundational skills never fully develop. Not because they lack ability, but because the environment they learned in didn't require those skills in the same way.

This isn't a reason to reject AI tools. It is a reason to be intentional about how they are used — particularly in the development of junior talent.

## What Good Looks Like

The engineering organisations navigating this most effectively are those that use AI tools as a multiplier for strong fundamentals rather than a substitute for them. They invest in deliberate practice, code review culture, and structured learning — while also embracing the genuine productivity gains that AI makes possible.

The talent gap is not inevitable. But avoiding it requires awareness, and it requires leaders who are willing to think carefully about what they are optimising for.
